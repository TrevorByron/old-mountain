# Olalashe Safaris — Website Design & Messaging Brief

## Brand context

Olalashe Safaris was founded in 2026 by brothers Jesse and Trevor Borden. They grew up in the Loita Hills of southern Kenya — remote Maasai land — straddling two worlds from childhood. In college they started informal safaris: campus flyers, summers guiding peers through dry riverbeds, on foot, off every tourist map. The company name comes from the Maasai word for brother. Two brothers. The brotherhood of guides and travelers. A bond with a people and a landscape. Introduce the name early. Let people feel its meaning without over-explaining it.

## Brand voice

Raw, earned, unhurried. No "curated experiences" or "bespoke itineraries." Write like a letter from someone who knows the place. Campfire stories, not hotel brochures. The Maasai world is present the way it's present in the landscape — not spotlighted, just woven in naturally.

If a line sounds like it could appear on a luxury travel site, rewrite it. If it sounds like a gap-year Instagram caption, rewrite it. Aim for something quieter and more specific than both.

**Words to use:** dust, on foot, dry season, off the map, brother, grew up here, real, small group, the Loita Hills, Maasai land, not the tourist track.

**Words to avoid:** curated, bespoke, luxury, authentic experience, bucket list, discover yourself, untouched wilderness, world-class, exclusive access.

## Headline directions

- **Option A:** "Olalashe means brother. We've been leading people into Maasai land since before we had a name for it."
- **Option B:** "We grew up here. Now we take people back."
- **Option C:** "Not a tour. Not a lodge. Two brothers and the land they know by heart."

The headline should land the origin story in one or two lines and make the reader want to ask "what's the story?"

## Page-by-page brief

**Homepage:** Open with full-bleed imagery — on foot, not from a vehicle. Red dust, a figure mid-walk, the Loita Hills. The headline establishes brothers and place. Below the fold: a short origin paragraph (3–5 sentences max), then one clear path forward. Do not show a trip grid on the homepage. Brand story before booking.

**About / The brothers:** The heart of the site. Jesse and Trevor's story, personal and specific. Name the places. Describe what it felt like to bridge two worlds. No mission statements. End with why "Olalashe" and what it means to them.

**Trips / Safaris:** Lead with philosophy before itinerary. Small groups, tailored to interests and abilities. Not a fixed-route conveyor. The trip shape changes depending on who's coming — biking, walking, rock climbing, hot air balloons, whatever fits. Each trip listing opens with a one-sentence scene that drops the reader into a moment. No bullet-point spec sheets.

**The place (Loita Hills / Maasai land):** Introduce the region and Maasai culture to someone who's never heard of either. Specific, respectful, present. Not Wikipedia, not a tourism board pamphlet. More like: here's the place we grew up in, here's why it matters.

**Inquire / Contact:** Short paragraph explaining this is a direct conversation with Jesse and Trevor. A form or email. No booking engine, no "select your package."

## Audience

Two groups, one voice. College-age travelers: speak to the campfire origin, the riverbeds-in-summer story, going somewhere that isn't on anyone's feed. Professionals 30s–50s: speak to depth and access, beyond the conventional safari, a guide who actually grew up there. The story speaks to both without splitting the voice.

## Visual direction

**Photography:** on foot, not vehicular. Dust, open sky, landscape texture, candid over posed. Maasai people appear naturally in context, not as visual props.

**Color:** earth tones, warm ochre, dry-grass yellows, deep African sky blue. No bright greens or gradients.

**Typography:** strong serif for brand name and headlines. No decorative or hand-lettered fonts.
